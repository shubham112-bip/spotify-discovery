## Raw notes

  - Goes straight to 90s playlist — "I know I'll enjoy it. I don't always have the patience to search for something"
  - Doesn't listen all day; limited windows mean he doesn't want to spend 10 minutes deciding
  - "I just want to press play and enjoy it"

- **Commute/Routine ritual:**
  - No commute listening — listening is tied to **evening wind-down** and **weekend downtime**
  - Post-dinner balcony routine: same 90s playlist while sitting or working on laptop
  - Weekend: slightly more open to exploration but still within 90s Hindi/English lanes

- **Repeat behavior & playlist replay:**
  - Few weeks ago: same playlist every evening for 5+ days during a hectic work period — "just wanted something familiar in the evening"
  - Repeat = nostalgia — "These songs remind me of school and college days. Even if I've heard them a hundred times, I still enjoy them"
  - Repeating "honestly, I'm okay with it"
  - Loop breaks when: bored (rare), or **family interruption** — "Sometimes the kids connect their phones and suddenly my playlist is gone"
  - **Key frame: Nostalgia provides infinite replay value** — unlike P03 who experiences staleness, P04's emotional connection to the era prevents decay

- **Discovery attempts & frustrations:**
  - Last attempt ~1 month ago: searched "90s songs like Kumar Sanu" — looking for **forgotten songs**, not new artists
  - Opened a few playlists → listened to a handful → added 2–3 songs "I hadn't heard in years"
  - Went right back to regular playlist — "It just felt more comfortable"
  - **Annoyance with remixes:** "Sometimes the playlist starts mixing in newer remixes or random songs that don't match the mood. That's the annoying part"
  - **Key distinction: He wants REDiscovery, not discovery** — surfacing songs from his era he's forgotten, not exposing him to new music

- **Spotify surfaces (Discover Weekly, DJ, etc.):**
  - **Discover Weekly:** "Mostly I forget about it" — recommendations "aren't really my type"
  - **Daily Mix:** Decent — stays close to existing taste
  - **Song Radio:** Okay when anchored to a liked song
  - **Spotify DJ:** Tried once or twice, didn't continue — "I'd rather just play my own playlist"

- **Idea validation / suggestions:**
  - Wants **time-of-day awareness**: "In the evening I want calm old songs. On weekends maybe suggest forgotten 90s tracks instead of pushing new music all the time"
  - **More patient than younger users:** gives a song 40–60 seconds total (20–30 sec extra beyond first impression) because "some older songs take a little time before the singing starts"
  - **Unprompted key insight:** "Spotify already knows I like old songs, but it could do a better job of bringing back songs I haven't heard in years instead of recommending the same popular ones over and over"

## 3 observations (behavior-focused)

1. **Nostalgia makes repeat listening decay-proof:** Unlike P03 who experiences staleness after weeks, Mihir's emotional connection to 90s music gives his playlists infinite replay value. The songs aren't just music — they're memory anchors to school and college. This fundamentally changes the discovery calculus: he doesn't *need* novelty to sustain engagement. Spotify's churn-prevention logic (introduce new content to prevent boredom) is misaligned for nostalgia-driven listeners.

2. **Rediscovery ≠ discovery — a new category:** Mihir's only successful "discovery" was finding songs he *already knew but forgot*. He searched "90s songs like Kumar Sanu" to recover lost memory, not to explore new artists. This is a distinct user need: **nostalgia mining** — algorithmically surfacing tracks from a user's generational era that aren't in their current library. Spotify has the catalog but no surface designed for "songs from your era you haven't played in years."

3. **Family as the only loop-breaker:** The external force that disrupts his playlist isn't social recommendation (P01) or internal staleness (P03) — it's family members physically taking over the speaker. This is a shared-device/social-listening dynamic unique to the home context, where control over the playlist is negotiated, not individual.

## Contradiction vs. AI review corpus

The AI corpus frames discovery failure as "algorithm gives the same songs" → users want *new* music. Mihir inverts this entirely: he wants the *same era*, just different songs from it that he's forgotten. The corpus complaint about "repetitive recommendations" actually splits into two distinct problems for different segments:

1. **Young active users (P01, P03):** Repetitive = "you keep suggesting my own library back to me" → want novelty within energy lane
2. **Nostalgia listeners (P04):** Repetitive = "you keep showing the popular 90s hits instead of deep cuts from my era" → want depth within era, not breadth across eras

The AI corpus collapses both into one complaint. They're fundamentally different needs requiring different solutions.

Also: Mihir is the first participant with a longer skip threshold (40–60 sec vs. 20–30 sec for others). This maps to his music style — 90s Bollywood has slower intros — and suggests skip thresholds are genre-dependent, not universal.

## Solution ideas mentioned by participant

*(Keep separate from problem signals — do not over-index)*

1. **Time-of-day curation:** Evening = calm old songs, weekend = forgotten 90s exploration. Simple calendar heuristic, no mood input needed.
2. **"Songs you forgot" surface:** Algorithmically mine the user's generational catalog for tracks they haven't played in years but would recognize — nostalgia mining rather than discovery.
3. **Remix/era filtering:** Option to exclude modern remixes or remasters from nostalgia playlists — keep the vibe temporally consistent.
