## 3 observations (behavior-focused)

1. **Pre-app mood decision:** Mood is determined by physical state (sleep, lateness) *before* opening Spotify. The app's job is to match an already-decided emotional need, not help her discover one. Scrolling in a neutral state → decision paralysis → default to yesterday's playlist.

2. **Social-only discovery trigger:** The only thing that breaks the repeat-playlist loop is social proof (friend recommendation, reel tag, movie buzz). Algorithmic recommendations have zero pull — she doesn't even remember Discover Weekly exists. Discovery is a *social* behavior, not an *algorithmic* one for her.

3. **30-second risk window is existential:** A bad recommendation in the first 30 seconds of a commute is not just annoying — it's an active mood disruption during a low-energy window. The cost of a miss outweighs the benefit of a hit, which makes defaulting to known playlists a rational risk-avoidance strategy.

## Contradiction vs. AI review corpus

The AI corpus framed repeat listening as a *frustration* ("Spotify keeps playing the same songs"). P01 frames it as a *deliberate coping strategy* — she *chooses* to repeat because it reduces cognitive load. The problem isn't that Spotify repeats; it's that Spotify has no way to *offer something worth breaking the loop for* at the right moment. Repeat listening is a feature of her workflow, not a bug.

Additionally, the corpus emphasizes "algorithm sameness" as a top complaint, but P01 doesn't even engage with algorithmic surfaces enough to complain about them — she has *opted out entirely*. The problem is upstream of algorithm quality; it's about **zero trust in algorithmic curation during low-energy moments**.
