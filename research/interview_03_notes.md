## Raw notes

- **Warm-up / Listening habits:**
  - Context = instant decision: "If I'm going to the gym, I instantly put on my gym playlist because I already know it'll get me in the mood"
  - For non-gym contexts (college, work): quick mood check → loud Brazilian phonk or familiar English songs
  - Spends less than a minute deciding — fastest decision-maker of all participants so far
  - Cares more about **vibe than artist** — "If a song gives me energy, I'll play it even if I don't know who made it"

- **Commute/Routine ritual:**
  - Daily listener tied to gym + college commute
  - Gym playlist = hardcoded routine; no deliberation required
  - Travel to college = mood-dependent selection but from known library

- **Repeat behavior & playlist replay:**
  - This week: gym playlist on repeat — Brazilian phonk, Travis Scott, Future, The Weeknd, Yeat — "Nothing new honestly, just the same songs that keep giving energy"
  - Exam period last month: same playlist almost every day — "didn't want to think about music, just wanted something that I knew would keep my focus"
  - Repeating "feels fine most of the time, I actually like knowing what's coming next"
  - **Key nuance vs. P01/P02:** Acknowledges staleness — "after a few weeks some songs stop hitting the same, then I realize I need something new"
  - Would switch if: completely bored, mood changes, or discovers a genuinely better playlist

- **Discovery attempts & frustrations:**
  - Few weeks ago: searched for new Brazilian phonk playlists because gym songs were getting repetitive
  - Opened couple of playlists → skipped through a lot of songs → added 5–6 to liked songs → went back to old playlist anyway
  - New songs "were okay but they didn't have the same feeling as the ones I already liked"
  - **Workout disruption risk:** "I don't want my workout mood to get ruined. If the song is slow or doesn't have a good drop, I instantly lose the vibe. I don't want to keep skipping every 20 seconds while lifting"
  - Discovery threshold is even shorter than commute — 20 seconds, not 30

- **Spotify surfaces (Discover Weekly, DJ, etc.):**
  - **Discover Weekly:** "Mostly I forget it exists" — recommendations don't match the *moment*; occasional good find
  - **Daily Mix:** Most trusted surface — "already close to what I like"
  - **Song Radio:** Decent when anchored to a specific liked song
  - **Spotify DJ:** "Tried it a few times but I don't really use it now. It talks too much and sometimes changes the mood completely"

- **Idea validation / suggestions:**
  - Wants automatic context detection: "If it's morning before gym, just give me hard-hitting tracks. If I'm coming back home after a long day, switch to chill English songs automatically"
  - Doesn't want to keep changing playlists himself
  - **Unprompted key insight:** "I don't like when Spotify keeps recommending the exact same songs again and again. Even if I want similar music, I still want a few fresh tracks mixed in without completely changing my vibe"
  - This is the clearest articulation of the **"safe discovery" sweet spot**: same energy lane, different tracks

---

## 3 observations (behavior-focused)

1. **Context-hardcoded playlists eliminate decision cost:** Harsh has solved the decision-fatigue problem P01 struggles with by creating a 1:1 mapping between context (gym) and playlist. There's zero deliberation for gym sessions. The problem only surfaces for ambiguous contexts (college commute, neutral mood) where scrolling paralysis kicks in — "I keep scrolling thinking I'll find something better, but after 3-4 minutes I get bored and just play one of my saved playlists."

2. **Staleness is the only crack in the repeat loop:** Unlike P01 and P02 who frame repeating as permanently fine, Harsh acknowledges a decay curve — songs "stop hitting the same" after a few weeks. He's the first participant to express *intrinsic* motivation for discovery (not social-triggered like P01, not phase-dependent like P02). But even when motivated, his discovery attempt (searching new phonk playlists) failed because the new songs didn't match the *feeling* of his curated set.

3. **20-second gym skip threshold is the harshest filter:** The gym context compresses the tolerance window from 30 seconds (commute) to 20 seconds. A bad recommendation during a lift isn't just annoying — it physically disrupts the workout flow. This makes gym listening the highest-stakes discovery context and explains why he'd rather loop a stale playlist than risk a mood-breaking interruption.

## Contradiction vs. AI review corpus

The AI corpus treats "repetitive recommendations" and "repeat listening" as the same complaint. Harsh separates them clearly: he *wants* to repeat his own curated music (that's intentional), but he *doesn't want* Spotify to recommend the same songs he's already heard. He wants **novelty within a fixed energy lane** — new Brazilian phonk that hits as hard as his current set, not a completely different genre. The corpus misses this distinction between user-driven repetition (good) and algorithm-driven repetition (bad).

Also notable: Harsh is the first participant to acknowledge that repeat listening has a natural expiry. P01 and P02 treat it as indefinitely comfortable. Harsh sees it as a *resource that depletes* — songs stop hitting — which creates a natural opening for discovery if it can match the energy precisely.

## Solution ideas mentioned by participant

*(Keep separate from problem signals — do not over-index)*

1. **Automatic time-of-day context switching:** Morning before gym = hard-hitting automatically; evening return = chill English automatically. Zero-input mode detection.
2. **"Fresh tracks in the same lane" injection:** Wants a few new songs mixed into familiar energy without changing the overall vibe — essentially a "safe discovery drip" within existing playlists.
3. **Vibe-first, artist-agnostic discovery:** Cares about energy/feel over artist identity — discovery surface should lead with vibe descriptors (BPM, energy, drop intensity) rather than artist similarity.
