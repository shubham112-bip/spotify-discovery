## 3 observations (behavior-focused)

1. **Music as spiritual infrastructure, not content consumption:** Ila's morning bhajan playlist functions like a prayer routine — the value is in the ritual repetition, not the novelty. This is fundamentally different from all other participants who use music as entertainment (even P04's nostalgia has an emotional-recreation element). For ritual listeners, any algorithmic intervention that disrupts the expected flow is experienced as a violation of sacred space, not just a mood mismatch.

2. **Speaker-based listening creates a zero-tolerance disruption model:** Because she plays on a speaker while doing housework, she can't quickly skip a bad recommendation. Every disruption requires physically walking to the device or phone. This raises the cost of a wrong song from "annoying" (headphone users who tap skip) to "flow-breaking" (must stop the chore to fix it). The ideal system for this user is one that **never needs intervention** — a set-it-and-forget-it playlist with guaranteed flow consistency.

3. **Event-driven discovery is the only discovery:** Her only search for new music was triggered by a specific event (family pooja before a festival). She didn't seek novelty from boredom or staleness — she needed *functionally specific* devotional music for a ritual occasion. After the event, she returned to her regular playlist. This suggests that for ritual listeners, discovery surfaces should be **occasion-triggered** ("Navratri is coming — here are garba tracks you might not know") rather than time-based or mood-based.

## Contradiction vs. AI review corpus

The AI corpus is built entirely on a **content consumption paradigm** — users want better recommendations to discover new music. Ila represents a **ritual use paradigm** where the app's job is to reliably play known, calming content without interruption. The corpus complaint "Spotify keeps recommending the same songs" is inverted here: she *wants* the same songs, and her complaint would be "Spotify keeps *disrupting* the same songs with remixes and random tracks."

This archetype challenges the project's framing. "Repeat listening" in the corpus is positioned as a problem to solve. For Ila, repeat listening *is* the product. The problem isn't that she repeats — it's that Spotify doesn't protect the purity of her ritual playlist from algorithmic contamination (remixes, genre-adjacent but tonally wrong suggestions).

Also parallels P04 strongly: both want rediscovery of forgotten songs within their genre, both hate remix intrusions, both have patient skip thresholds. But Ila's context is spiritual rather than nostalgic, and her speaker-based listening creates uniquely high disruption costs.

## Solution ideas mentioned by participant

*(Keep separate from problem signals — do not over-index)*

1. **Flow-protected playlists:** Guarantee energy-level consistency within a playlist — no sudden volume/tempo/style shifts. Flag and exclude remixes from devotional contexts.
2. **Occasion-triggered suggestions:** "Navratri is coming — here are devotional songs you might not know" rather than weekly algorithmic pushes. Event-calendar-aware curation.
3. **"Forgotten devotional songs" surface:** Like P04's nostalgia mining but for spiritual music — surface old bhajans from her era/tradition she hasn't encountered on the platform.
4. **Speaker-optimized mode:** A "set and forget" mode that guarantees zero disruptions — no ads if premium, no tonal shifts, no autoplay into unrelated content.
